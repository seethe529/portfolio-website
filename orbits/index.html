Orbital Shell Overview

Overview

The orbital shell visualization demonstrates how near-Earth space can be divided into structured orbital tracts — 3D spatial zones that represent Low Earth Orbit (LEO), Medium Earth Orbit (MEO), and Geostationary Orbit (GEO). Each shell highlights a distinct functional layer of Earth's orbital environment, illustrating how spatial zoning and geospatial modeling can be extended beyond Earth's surface into orbital space.
These orbital tracts form the foundation of a conceptual space geography system — a framework for visualizing, indexing, and managing orbital traffic with the same precision used for terrestrial mapping. The design bridges orbital mechanics and GIS, allowing analysts, engineers, and policymakers to better understand how altitude, inclination, and longitudinal position define operational regions in space.
🛰️ LEO (Low Earth Orbit)
LEO-A1000-I0-RAAN0_5
Satellite Example: Starlink-3015 — operating within a ~550 km altitude band, transiting multiple LEO tracts for global internet coverage.
Use Case: Demonstrates how dense satellite constellations can be spatially indexed across altitude and inclination segments, enabling tract-based monitoring, collision avoidance, and orbital population analytics.
🛰️ MEO (Medium Earth Orbit)
MEO-A20200-I56-RAAN180_185
Satellite Example: GPS IIF-5 — operating at ~20,200 km altitude within a fixed inclination band to maintain global navigation coverage.
Use Case: Shows how navigation satellites can be grouped within MEO tracts for precise orbital slot management, time synchronization analysis, and constellation geometry modeling.
🛰️ GEO (Geostationary Orbit)
GEO-TOROIDAL-RAAN0_30-T1-P1
Satellite Example: GOES-16 — stationed at ~35,786 km altitude above the equator, providing continuous weather and environmental monitoring for the Western Hemisphere.
Use Case: Illustrates how geostationary tracts can be used for regulatory coordination, debris mitigation planning, and long-term space situational awareness.
Closing Statement

These examples show how orbital tracts can be used to spatially classify and analyze satellite operations — transforming orbital mechanics into a mappable, policy-ready geospatial framework. The visualization reflects Ryan Charles Lingo's ongoing research in orbital zoning and space sustainability under his Extra-Orbital Solutions initiative.
