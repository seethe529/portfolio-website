Why Orbital Governance Matters

January 15, 2025 Reflection 7 min read
Governance Policy Space

As I watch the night sky from my backyard, I'm struck by a sobering reality: what appears as peaceful darkness is actually one of the most congested and contested domains on Earth. With over 4,000 active satellites currently orbiting our planet—and projections suggesting this number could reach 100,000 within the next decade—we're witnessing an unprecedented expansion into near-Earth space that demands immediate governance attention.

The Scale of the Challenge

The numbers tell a compelling story. SpaceX's Starlink constellation alone plans to deploy 42,000 satellites. Amazon's Project Kuiper aims for 3,236. OneWeb, Telesat, and dozens of other companies are racing to establish their presence in Low Earth Orbit (LEO). This isn't just about internet connectivity—it's about establishing territorial claims in a domain that, until recently, was the exclusive province of superpowers.

"Space is not infinite. The orbital real estate we're fighting over represents some of the most valuable territory in human history—and we're dividing it up with 20th-century frameworks."

The current regulatory framework, built around the 1967 Outer Space Treaty, was designed for a world where only a handful of nations could reach orbit. Today, private companies launch more satellites in a single mission than entire countries deployed in the first decades of the space age. We're operating under governance structures that predate the internet, trying to manage a domain that's becoming as critical to global infrastructure as undersea cables or GPS.

Beyond Collision Avoidance

Most discussions about space governance focus on collision avoidance—and rightfully so. The Kessler Syndrome, where cascading collisions create debris fields that make entire orbital zones unusable, represents an existential threat to our space-based infrastructure. But orbital governance encompasses far more than just keeping satellites from crashing into each other.

Consider the economic implications. A single geostationary orbital slot can generate billions in telecommunications revenue. The GPS constellation provides $1.4 trillion in annual economic benefits to the U.S. economy alone. When we talk about orbital governance, we're talking about managing resources that underpin modern civilization.

The Transparency Problem

One of the most pressing challenges is the lack of transparency in orbital operations. While the U.S. Space Force tracks objects larger than 10 centimeters, there's no comprehensive, real-time database of satellite intentions, capabilities, or operational status that's accessible to all stakeholders. This opacity creates several problems:

  • Collision Risk: Without knowing a satellite's planned maneuvers, other operators can't effectively coordinate avoidance strategies
  • Spectrum Interference: Radio frequency coordination requires understanding not just where satellites are, but what they're doing
  • Regulatory Compliance: National regulators struggle to enforce licensing conditions when they can't monitor real-time operations
  • Insurance and Risk Assessment: The space insurance industry operates with incomplete information about the assets they're covering

The Segmentation Solution

This is where the concept of orbital segmentation becomes crucial. Just as we divide terrestrial airspace into controlled zones with specific rules and responsibilities, we need to think about near-Earth space as a three-dimensional geography that can be mapped, zoned, and governed.

The Orbital Tract Framework I've been developing represents one approach to this challenge. By dividing orbital space into discrete, mappable volumes defined by altitude, inclination, and right ascension, we create a foundation for more granular governance. Each "tract" becomes a spatial unit that can be assigned specific rules, monitored for compliance, and managed for optimal utilization.

Equitable Access and the Commons

Perhaps the most important aspect of orbital governance is ensuring equitable access. The current system essentially operates on a "first-come, first-served" basis, which inherently favors wealthy nations and corporations. This approach risks creating a space-based version of the digital divide, where developing nations are permanently locked out of the most valuable orbital real estate.

We need governance frameworks that balance commercial innovation with global equity. This might involve:

  • Reserved orbital zones for developing nations and international scientific missions
  • Progressive licensing fees that fund space debris cleanup and developing nation space programs
  • Mandatory technology sharing requirements for operators using premium orbital slots
  • International oversight of mega-constellation deployments

The Path Forward

Effective orbital governance won't emerge from traditional diplomatic channels alone. The pace of commercial space development far exceeds the speed of international treaty negotiations. Instead, we need hybrid approaches that combine:

Technical Standards: Industry-developed protocols for orbital coordination, similar to how internet protocols evolved through technical working groups rather than government mandate.

Data Transparency: Open-source tracking and coordination systems that provide real-time orbital awareness to all stakeholders, not just military space surveillance networks.

Economic Incentives: Market-based mechanisms that reward responsible orbital behavior and penalize activities that increase collision risk or debris generation.

Adaptive Regulation: Governance frameworks designed to evolve with technology, rather than static treaties that become obsolete within decades.

Why This Matters Now

The decisions we make about orbital governance in the next five years will shape humanity's relationship with space for generations. We're at a critical juncture where we can either establish sustainable, equitable frameworks for space utilization, or we can continue with ad hoc approaches that virtually guarantee future conflicts and environmental disasters.

The stakes couldn't be higher. Our GPS navigation, weather forecasting, climate monitoring, global communications, and financial systems all depend on space-based infrastructure. As we become increasingly dependent on orbital assets, the need for robust governance becomes not just important—it becomes existential.

The question isn't whether we need orbital governance. The question is whether we'll develop it proactively, through thoughtful planning and international cooperation, or reactively, in response to catastrophic failures that could have been prevented.

I believe we still have time to choose the former. But that window is closing rapidly, measured not in decades but in launch cycles.
